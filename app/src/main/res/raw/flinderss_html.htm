Flinders Street Station



Flinders Street is Australia’s oldest train station with its distinguishing yellow disguise and green copper ceiling; it is easily a present day icon of Melbourne located in the City Centre. It is a central meeting place for heaps of commuters and was also regarded as Melbourne’s premier gathering place, picking the tag 'meet me under the clocks'.Flinders Street Station is one of the major landmarks of the Melbourne CBD.



Flinders Street Station was formally released to the public in 1910 with initial railway lines servicing Werribee, Dandenong, Aspendale and Sandringham. Towards the 1930s, Flinders Street Station typically was commuting 241,130 passengers per week, placing it much in front of any other train station in the world at that time, be it Gare Saint-Lazare, Paris (which was servicing 150,000 passengers per week ) or the famous Grand Central Station, New York (108,843 passengers per week).



Flinders Street is undoubtedly the most demanding central railway station in the southern hemisphere, with more than 1500 trains and 110,000 customers transitioning through it every day. As per its citation on the Victorian Heritage Register, its 708-metre main platform is the fourth lengthiest railway platform in the world.



In 1854, Thousands of people gathered at the station and beside the track to Sandridge (known today as Port Melbourne) to see not just Victoria's, but Australia's first public steam train. Princes Bridge and Spencer Street City Stations were released in 1859. In the late 1880s, Flinders Street Station trains started services to Port Melbourne and St Kilda; Brighton Beach and Hawthorn trains proceeded from Princes Bridge Station; and Southern Cross Station(Previously known as Spencer Street Station) gratified for trains serving Williamstown, Geelong, Ballarat, Woodend, Kyneton, Sandhurst (renamed Bendigo), Echuca and Wodonga.
